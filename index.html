Not necessarily — but it helps enormously. You can start as a manual tester without writing a single line of code. That's how many QA engineers, including myself, began their journey.

However, those who learn to code tend to contribute more, advance faster, and earn higher salaries over time. I personally picked up Java and Selenium after starting as a manual tester. My advice: start without code if you must, but commit to learning it alongside your QA fundamentals.

Here are five things that will make a real difference:

  1. Build a solid foundation — learn QA fundamentals, terminology, and core tools.
  2. Craft a strong, ATS-optimised CV that mirrors job descriptions.
  3. Keep your LinkedIn active — post your learnings, certifications, and projects.
  4. Research the tools used by real QA engineers and add them to your skillset.
  5. Apply broadly, including internships — each application teaches you something valuable.

If you're detail-oriented, curious, and genuinely care about user experience, QA is a great fit. Great testers tend to be creative thinkers who enjoy breaking things in order to make them better.

Even without a technical background you can break into QA with dedication and the right guidance. The field welcomes people from diverse backgrounds — what matters most is your commitment to quality and continuous learning.

With focused effort, most beginners are interview-ready within 2–3 months of following a structured path — completing the core courses, building a targeted CV, and actively applying.

The timeline depends on your consistency, how much time you invest daily, and the job market in your area. Internships are a great entry point; many convert into full-time roles.
