Build a CV That Gets Noticed

Your CV is your first impression. A well-structured, honest, and relevant CV will dramatically increase the number of interview calls you receive.

Essential CV Sections

  • Contact Information — Full name, phone, email, LinkedIn profile, and location (city is enough).
  • Professional Summary — 2–3 sentences describing who you are, your key skills, and what you bring to a team.
  • Skills — List relevant testing tools, methodologies, and programming languages. Be honest.
  • Work Experience — For each role: company name, your title, dates, and 3–5 bullet points of what you achieved (not just what you did).
  • Education — Degree, institution, graduation year.
  • Certifications — ISTQB Foundation Level is highly valued. Add any other relevant certifications.

Tips for a Strong QA CV

  • Tailor your CV to every job description — use the same keywords the job posting uses.
  • Use numbers where possible: "Reduced regression testing time by 40% by implementing Selenium automation."
  • List the tools you know: Jira, TestRail, Postman, Selenium, Cypress, Appium, etc.
  • Keep it to 1–2 pages. Recruiters spend an average of 6 seconds on a CV.
  • Use a clean, simple format. No tables, no columns — ATS systems often can't parse them.
  • Include a link to your LinkedIn profile — make sure it matches your CV.

Common CV Mistakes to Avoid

  • Typos and grammatical errors — always proofread and ask someone else to review.
  • Using generic objectives like "seeking a challenging role" — replace with a specific professional summary.
  • Listing responsibilities instead of achievements — focus on impact, not tasks.
  • Including irrelevant experience — every line should add value.
  • Using a photo (in most countries, especially for tech roles, this is unnecessary and can introduce bias).

For Freshers (No Experience)

Don't panic if you have no professional QA experience. Here's how to fill the gap:

  • Complete free or paid QA courses (Udemy, Coursera, Ministry of Testing).
  • Contribute to open-source testing projects on GitHub.
  • Create your own test projects — test a public website and document your findings.
  • Get an ISTQB Foundation Level certification.
  • Volunteer for QA work at local NGOs or small businesses.
